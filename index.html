Learning Socially Compatible Autonomous Driving

Introduction

In order to recognize and react appropriately to the driving intentions of human drivers, autonomous vehicles (AVs) must conduct extensive assessments. The inability of AVs to recognize the signs of cooperation offered by human drivers can have a negative effect on the transportation system by causing safety and efficiency issues. This study presents an architecture that integrates social psychology factors into the framework of AVs in an effort to offer a viable solution to this challenge. The main findings make use of social value orientation (SVO), a method for predicting driving behavior and evaluating the degree of cooperation among human drivers. An architecture based on deep reinforcement learning (DRL) with SVO integration was used to model social compatibility among road agents while taking safety regulations into consideration. To address the aforementioned concern, this study creates an architecture capable of capturing motion-controlling and socially compatible controls, performing well in complex interaction scenarios, and operating in accordance with the actions of other drivers. SVO integration proved to be an effective method of improving motion and interactive behaviors. Furthermore, the defined mechanism has a significant outcome when compared to motion control standards by outperforming every motion and socially compatible metrics.

Results

Learned social behavior

• Multi-behavior: Driving at a multitasking level to acquire various degrees of socially compatible driving under altruistic, prosocial, individualistic, and competitive preferences.

Non-social behavior

• Baseline (Non-social behavior): Only focusing on motion control policy.

Learned alruistic-social behavior

• Altrustic: The AV attempts to maximize the reward of the other drivers without considering its own outcomes.

Learned prosocial-social behavior

• Prosocial: The AV attempts to maximize the group reward, factoring in both its own outcome and the outcomes of others.

Learned individualistic-social behavior

• Individualistic: When the AV attempts to maximize its own outcome disregarding other drivers' outcomes, the individualistic behavior is observed.

Learned competitive-social behavior

• Competitive: When the AV attempts to maximize its own relative gain over others, competitive behaviors can be observed.

As a baseline driving policy, we utilize the provided default Deep Q-Learning (DQN), which does not consider social compatibility (Leurent, 2020). We modify the default MDP and make it socially compatible with our approach. To assess the social compatibility levels of the AV trained using our method, we define a few evaluation metrics.

  • Success rate (SR): The success rate indicates how successful the AV is in accomplishing its main objective. In our evaluation setting, the main objective is to drive without collisions.
  • Impacted velocity cases (IVC): This metric captures the impact of the velocities of other vehicles during an overtaking event by the AV. The number of impacted velocity cases is counted.
  • Braking impact (BI): This metric captures the breaking changes of other vehicles caused by the AV's actions.
  • Close gap front vehicle (CGF) and close-gap rear vehicle (CGR): These two metrics captures the number of occurrences such that the AV is too close to the front and rear vehicles.
  • Overtaking close gap front vehicle (OCGF) and Overtaking close gap rear vehicle (OCGR): These two metrics refer to the number of occurrences such that the AV is too close to the front and rear vehicles when the AV is attempting an overtaking event.
  • Human social preference (HSP) and AV social preference(AVSP): These two metrics can be used to evaluate how well model configuration and training were carried out.
